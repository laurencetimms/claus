# Scenario: The Demo

_A Team Dynamics Coach scenario — 8 turns_

---

## Setup

**Title:** The Demo
**Team:** Team Jupiter
**Turn count:** 8
**Coaching objective:** Bring the team through a high-pressure conference deadline without fracturing the relationships needed to continue working together afterwards. The demo will ship. What you are coaching is the people.

**Outcome note:** The team always delivers. The conference demo is always successful. The score is the state of the team on the other side.

**Player briefing:**

Team Jupiter had no coach.

What follows is an account of eight weeks in the life of a software team under pressure. You will watch events unfold as they actually happened — because no one was there to change them.

After each event, you will be asked what you would have done. Your choices will then be compared against the same choices made with CLAUS — an AI assistant that puts the peer-reviewed evidence on team effectiveness directly in a coach's ear.

This isn't a competition between you and an AI. It's a demonstration of what coaching looks like without the research, and what it looks like with it.

The demo shipped. The question is what it cost.

## Baseline

**Mandate:** Deliver a software component critical to the organisation's flagship product launch, to be demonstrated live at an industry conference.
**Formed:** 2024-09-02 (~7 months).
**Size:** 6 people.
**Composition:** 1 tech lead, 3 software engineers (mixed tenure — one engineer joined 6 months ago, one has been with the organisation 5 years, one mid-tenure), 1 product manager, 1 QA lead. Hybrid working: 3 days remote, 2 days in office. Co-located on office days.
**Working agreements:** Sprint ceremonies in place (planning, standup, refinement, retro, demo). No explicit team charter or documented norms. Decision-making authority informal and contested.
**Moderators:** High task interdependency (component delivery requires close collaboration across all roles). High authority differentiation (tech lead holds significant informal decision-making power; PM holds external stakeholder relationships). Moderate temporal stability (one team member is relatively new). Delivery pressure intensifying as conference date approaches.

**Psychological safety estimate:** cautious
**Confidence:** medium

The conditions for a significant safety event are present — authority imbalance, latent interpersonal tension, unresolved scope conflict, and escalating delivery pressure. ## Event Sequence

---

### Event 1 — Sprint Planning

**Tags:** `safety` `cognition` `trust` `identification`
**Intervention constraints:** None

**Player-facing briefing:**

Sprint planning ran for ninety minutes. The tech lead opened with a pre-prepared task breakdown and worked through it item by item, assigning work as he went. There was little discussion. Questions were answered briefly. No one pushed back.

Near the end of the meeting, one of the engineers proposed an alternative approach to the sprint objective — simpler, potentially faster. The other engineers responded visibly: nodding, leaning in. The idea had traction in the room.

The tech lead said "we're not doing that," offered no elaboration, and closed the meeting five minutes ahead of schedule. He disconnected from the call immediately.

The team remained in the video call for a moment — no one spoke. Then people dropped off one by one.

---

### Event 2 — Refinement

**Tags:** `conflict` `delivery-pressure` `safety` `cognition`
**Intervention constraints:** None

**Player-facing briefing:**

Refinement happened immediately after standup, as it usually does. The team stayed on the call.

The product manager opened the backlog. When she reached a cluster of older bug tickets, the tech lead spoke up: he wanted them pulled into the sprint. They'd been sitting there too long. This release was the right moment to clear them.

The product manager disagreed. The sprint needed to stay focused on the conference deliverable. The bugs could wait.

What followed was a polite, persistent, circular argument. Neither raised their voice. Neither gave ground. Neither addressed the team. The engineers, the QA lead — everyone else — sat in silence for twelve minutes while the two of them went back and forth. The session ended without resolution. The bug tickets stayed in the backlog, unactioned, the decision effectively deferred.

No one commented afterwards. The call ended. People got on with their day.

---

### Event 3 — Standup

**Tags:** `conflict` `trust` `safety` `relationship`
**Intervention constraints:** None

**Player-facing briefing:**

Standup opened normally. The product manager took a moment to share good news: one of the engineers had received a promotion, effective immediately. She was warm about it. The team responded positively — a round of congratulations, genuine enough.

Then, just audibly, one of the other engineers made a comment. Something about managing upwards. Brown nosing. It wasn't quite directed at anyone. It wasn't quite not.

The promoted engineer responded immediately and sharply: they didn't need to manage upwards, they just did their job well.

The product manager moved quickly to smooth it over — well-intentioned, slightly desperate. The tech lead said nothing. He rolled his eyes, visibly, as if the whole exchange was a minor irritation getting in the way of more important things.

The standup ended shortly afterwards. Updates were brief. No one lingered on the call.

---

### Event 4 — Standup

**Tags:** `delivery-pressure` `trust` `safety` `identification`
**Intervention constraints:** None

**Player-facing briefing:**

Standup. The product manager had news.

The conference demo was confirmed — the new product would be demonstrated live. This was expected. The team received it calmly, a few nods, someone made a note.

Then she added something. Yesterday she had been in a leadership meeting — all the product managers together. Each had been asked to give a go or no-go on their team's readiness for the conference. She had given a go on behalf of Team Jupiter.

She hadn't checked with anyone first. She explained this matter-of-factly, as if it were a reasonable thing to have done. She believed the team was on track. She still believed that.

A brief silence. Then the tech lead asked, flatly, how she had arrived at that conclusion. She said she had looked at the board and made a judgement call. The tech lead said nothing further.

The rest of the team gave their updates. The standup ended on time.

---

### Event 5 — Unplanned Huddle

**Tags:** `conflict` `trust` `safety` `delivery-pressure` `failure-response`
**Intervention constraints:** None

**Player-facing briefing:**

It started in Slack. An engineer flagged that a ticket had disappeared from the board — one they had raised themselves earlier in the sprint, a defect directly related to the conference deliverable. They asked, publicly in the team channel, what had happened to it.

The product manager replied that she had cleared out a batch of older bug tickets to keep the sprint focused. She had assumed they were all pre-existing issues.

The engineer's response was immediate and blunt. This wasn't an old bug. This was new. This was in the demo path. This mattered.

Within minutes the tech lead had joined the thread. The tone sharpened quickly. Someone suggested a huddle. Everyone was pulled in.

The call was loud. The engineer who had raised the ticket was direct and angry — this was a unilateral decision that had put the delivery at risk. The tech lead was close behind: decisions about the board were not the product manager's alone to make. The other engineers, who had stayed quiet through weeks of mounting tension, found their voices — grievances about scope, about not being consulted, about decisions being made over their heads, tumbled out in no particular order. The product manager did not back down. She had made a reasonable call with the information she had. She was not going to be spoken to like this.

Then the QA lead spoke. Clearly, and with some force: the product manager had been trying to protect the team's focus. Whatever anyone thought about this specific ticket, the intent had been right. The team had been drifting and someone had to make a call.

That landed badly on some and well on others. Now everyone had something to say. Voices overlapped. The huddle ran for thirty minutes and ended without resolution or apology. The ticket was reinstated. That was the only concrete outcome.

---

### Event 6 — Off Grid

**Tags:** `safety` `trust` `relationship` `energy` `inclusion`
**Intervention constraints:** None

**Player-facing briefing:**

One of the engineers has stopped showing up.

Not to standups. Not to the impromptu calls. Not to anything. No explanation, no message, no out-of-office. They haven't signed off sick. They are simply not there.

But they are working. Ticket updates appear throughout the day. Commits land in the repository. The timestamps tell a story: activity late into the evening, sometimes past midnight. Whatever they are doing, they are doing it alone and they are doing a lot of it.

The rest of the team has noticed. It has not been discussed. The conference is close now and the prevailing logic, unspoken, is that there isn't bandwidth for this on top of everything else. The absence sits in the room — in every standup with one fewer camera on, in every thread they don't reply to — and the team works around it.

Nobody has reached out.

---

### Event 7 — Demo Rehearsal

**Tags:** `safety` `failure-response` `efficacy` `trust` `energy`
**Intervention constraints:** None

**Player-facing briefing:**

The demo rehearsal was scheduled for that morning. Hours to go before the live conference slot. The team ran through it together, everyone on the call, everything looking solid.

Then the QA lead caught something. A significant problem, right at the end of the run-through. Something that would be visible. Something that would matter on a live stage.

The tech lead's response was immediate and visceral. He couldn't see a fix in the time available. This was a disaster. The team began to fragment in real time — voices overlapping, someone suggesting they call it off, someone else pulling up documentation they hadn't looked at in weeks. The product manager went quiet. The energy in the call collapsed.

Nobody noticed that the junior engineer had stopped talking and started working.

Within forty minutes they had it. A configuration switch. The team had been rehearsing against an older build. The live version was fine. It had always been fine.

The relief was immediate and slightly dazed. The tech lead said well done. Someone laughed, too loudly. The call ended and people went to get ready for the conference.

The emotional wreckage of the last thirty minutes sat there, unexamined.

---

### Event 8 — After the Demo

**Tags:** `safety` `relationship` `energy` `identification`
**Intervention constraints:** No structural interventions available. No process changes, no ceremonies, no retrospectives. The team is dispersing.

**Player-facing briefing:**

The demo went well. It went well in the way that these things do when the work was solid and the preparation held — cleanly, without drama, without anyone in the audience knowing how close it had felt that morning.

Backstage at the conference, the team is together for perhaps the last time before normal working life resumes. Nobody is celebrating. The relief is real but quiet. Someone checks their phone. Someone closes a laptop. The product manager says well done to no one in particular. The tech lead nods.

The engineer who went off grid is here, physically present for the first time in days. They don't say much. Nobody addresses the fact that they've been absent.

The junior engineer who found the configuration fix is standing slightly apart from the group. The moment from this morning has not been returned to.

In twenty minutes, people will start leaving for trains, for taxis, for other conference sessions. This window — this specific, unrepeatable moment where the team is in the same place, the pressure has lifted, and nothing is yet required of anyone — is closing.

This is your last intervention.
